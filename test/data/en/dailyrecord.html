Family of brave Scots hostage reveal he smiled at his killers as they shot him in gas plant siege

22 Jan 2013 00:01

KENNY WHITESIDE'S family found out he had been murdered in the gas plant siege after an Algerian friend posted a message on Facebook.

HOSTAGE Scot Kenny Whiteside stared his murderers in the eye and smiled as they shot him dead.

Survivors of the Algerian gas plant siege saw dad-of-two Kenny’s defiance as terrorists killed him and three of his pals.

His tearful brother-in-law John Strachan said yesterday: “An Algerian colleague saw him executed.

“He didn’t beg for his life, and he was looking his killers straight in the eye when they shot him.

“He was that type of man – solid, reliable and as brave as they come.”

Kenny, 59, originally from Auchmuty in Fife, is one of six Britons dead or missing, feared dead after the horrific siege at the In Amenas gas plant.

Thirty-seven foreign workers and one Algerian were murdered and five foreigners are missing, feared dead. Twenty-nine militants died as Algerian troops stormed the site.

In the chaos of the siege, Kenny’s family found out he was dead after looking at Facebook.

The Algerian friend who saw him die put a message on the site, in French, after escaping the carnage.

Kenny’s wife Catherine and a friend who spoke French saw the post and got in touch.

George Mcluskie
 

The Algerian told them that Kenny had been held with three other foreigners and a number of local workers in a room at the plant.

When the terrorists holding the four foreigners heard gunfire from another part of the site, they immediately shot them at close range.

British consular officials visited the family on Saturday at their home in Johannesburg, South Africa, and confirmed Kenny was among the dead.

He had been identified from witness testimony, photos and documents. The officials took DNA from Kenny’s daughters, Alana, 27, and Nova, 25, as part of the formalities for returning his body to the family.

Cruelly, the family’s hopes had been raised hours earlier by a false newspaper report claiming Kenny had got out alive.

John, from Glenrothes, said: “A friend in Scotland phoned Catherine to tell her about the story on Saturday morning and she immediately called the paper’s head office to find out where they’d got the information from.

George Mcluskie
 

“They refused to tell her and said they’d call back. They still haven’t phoned.

“Catherine and the girls got their hopes up and their phone was ringing off the hook from people congratulating them.

“We were ecstatic when we saw the article too, but that all changed a few hours later.

“The heartbreak is compounded because people still think he got out alive. People keep asking me when he’s coming home and if we’re having a party.”

Kenny’s murder is the second tragedy in weeks to affect his family. He last returned to Scotland over Christmas for the funeral of his brother Jim, 69.

It’s understood Jim died in hospital shortly after a blood transfusion.

Miner’s son Kenny moved to South Africa in 1981 immediately after marrying Catherine, and both their girls were born there. But he never forgot his roots and returned to Scotland four times a year.

He was also a dedicated member of the Tartan Army, often playing his bagpipes at games.

John said: “Kenny was a hard-working, diligent man who did everything for Catherine and the girls. He absolutely doted
on his family.

“Alana and Nova had him around their little fingers and he loved it.

“He made sure they never wanted for anything. He worked hard and kept doing courses to constantly better himself.

“He got on with everyone and played football and squash with the local workers in Algeria. It’s no surprise they are speaking very highly of him.”

Kenny was working as a project services planner for Norwegian oil company Rovin at the In Amenas plant. He had also worked in Canada and Russia.

John refused to blame his brother-in-law’s death on the Algerian special forces who took on the terrorists. He said: “It’s difficult to comment when we have no real idea what happened.

George Mcluskie
 

“There was no way the terrorists were going to release anybody. I think they intended to kill the hostages from the start.

“The Algerian soldiers were very brave and risked their lives doing what they thought was best.

“They were in a Catch 22 situation. The plant was huge, there were pockets of hostages everywhere. Any rescue attempt would have been nigh on impossible.”

Kenny’s brother Bob, 67, of Crieff, Perthshire, said he held no grudges against the Algerian authorities. He added: “That’s their system, and they weren’t bothered about the hostages as such.

“They just wanted to get the plant clear of all the terrorists.”

Bob said police came to his home on Sunday evening to confirm Kenny had been killed, but he and wife Stephanie already knew by then.

He added: “We had to find out for ourselves. We were not given any official information.”

DAVID CAMERON warned MPs yesterday of a “generational struggle” to defeat al-Qaeda terrorism in Saharan Africa.

He pledged additional British support for the French operation against the fanatics in Mali. He said numbers would be in the “tens rather than hundreds” but refused to rule out combat troops.
