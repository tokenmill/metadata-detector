Singapore growing as precious metals trading hub

As trade agency IE Singapore pushes ahead with efforts to develop Singapore as a precious metals trading hub, industry players say they are benefiting from more growth and trade opportunities.

  • By Nicole Tan
  • Posted 17 Nov 2015 00:10
  • Updated 17 Nov 2015 00:38

SINGAPORE:Of all the gold and silver stored by bullion retailer Silver Bullion, about 90 per cent is owned by foreigners. Silver Bullion said Singapore is seen by overseas buyers as a secure jurisdiction for storing their precious metals.

On top of strong global demand, it said growth in the domestic industry gave a lift to sales in recent years - in particular, the removal of 7 per cent goods and services tax for investment precious metals in 2012.

Said Silver Bullion founder Gregor Gregersen: "Investment with 7 per cent tax is hard to work so (its removal) created much broader appeal and allowed people to buy and sell it much more easily. The next thing that IE did was it ensured that we have a refinery coming to Singapore. So Metalor came. And a refinery provides liquidity for the physical metal. So it makes it easier to have large amounts of gold being bought and sold."

PUSH TO BUILD SINGAPORE AS PRECIOUS METALS TRADING HUB PAYING OFF

IE Singapore has been pushing to build up Singapore as a precious metals trading hub.

Singapore already has the infrastructure and broad network of companies due to its strength as a commodities trading hub - in particular, the energy sector. Now, according to IE Singapore, the focus is on bringing in top global players in the metals industry.

"For precious metals specifically, we look at what's needed in precious metals hub. We benchmark hubs already exist in the world, decide what type of hub we want it to be, what Singapore already has and what else we need to pull in to make it a complete hub. We wanted to be a physical hub. So we pulled in a world-class refinery from Switzerland – Metalor," said Ms Gina Lim, group director for Trade Services and Policy at IE Singapore.

"We're seeing good growth from entry of companies, especially from Southeast Asia. We're in talks with quite a few traders around the region working to use Singapore as a base for their operations. Being in a hub allows them to be close to information, and also in the event of default, they could find other options to get rid of goods or to buy from someone else," she added.

In 2012, Swiss-based commodities trading firm Trafigura shifted its headquarters and main trading centre to Singapore, and Mitsubishi Corp, too, moved its global metals headquarters to the city-state.

Commodities trading firm MRI Group said the growing network of such companies in Singapore presents further growth opportunities.

"When you have the aggregation of more players, it becomes a hub. We started to grow. So that did increase the depth of the market, and hence there was a lot more opportunity to trade. So for us, we are lucky that we're part of that trend, in terms of the growth of the business," said Mr Alan Kuek, CEO of MRI.

"For MRI, we do supplement the trading with the backing of financial institution, so we provide financial solutions for our suppliers and our buyers. Typically, we do help our suppliers or buyers to structure some form of financing that could enable them to mitigate the businesses they're running, and still supply the products we need, or buyers to buy our product," he added.

According to IE Singapore, about 80 per cent of the top global commodity firms operate in Singapore, and the sector employs about 14,600 people.
