That 'Who's Who' invite aims at your ego -- and your wallet: Plain Dealing

By Sheryl Harris, The Plain Dealer The Plain Dealer
on February 12, 2013 at 2:00 PM, updated February 13, 2013 at 11:14 AM
Print
View/Post Comments
Don't let that Who's Who invite go to your head. Associated Press file  

Don’t feel too special if you’re invited to be in a “Who’s Who” directory.

It could be a vanity scam.

There are legitimate Who’s Who directories, but just like the phrase “Yellow Pages,” “Who’s Who” is a term in the public domain, meaning anyone can use it – including scammers.

In “who’s who” scams, fly-by-night companies tell people they’ve been chosen to be included – or to see if they qualify for inclusion — in a directory of important people.

In some cases, people are asked to pay a fee to be listed or to join the organization publishing the book.

Often though, the companies make their money selling the books to the people listed for hundreds of dollars apiece.

That’s a lot to pay for a book whose only market is the people in it.

Really, you’ll never find most of these directories at libraries.

Once scammers hook people for the cost of the book, they then try to sell them expensive commemorative plaques and other useless trophies.

Red flags include:

  • Generic congratulations or invitation emails. They’re spam.

  • Requests for a credit card during the listing process. If someone asks for your credit card number, they plan to charge you.
  • Requests for detailed personal information that could put you at risk of identity theft.
  • Emails from foreign email addresses (.uk, .au, etc.)

If you can’t tell whether the offer is legitimate, check out the publisher with the Better Business Bureau report at bbb.org/us.
